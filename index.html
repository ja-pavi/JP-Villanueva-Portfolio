Introduction to Convertible Arbitrage

written by Jacob Villanueva

Introduction

Convertible arbitrage is an investment strategy that consists of two key parts: the purchase of a convertible and the shorting of its underlying stock. Convertibles serve the same function as a corporate bond with the one key difference being the ability to convert the convertible into a fixed number of shares of common stock. Due to this key feature, convertible arbitrage becomes a possibility.

Definition of Arbitrage

The definition of arbitrage that I like most comes from Sheldon Natenberg's Options Volatility & Pricing novel where he states the definition of arbitrage as, "The purchase and sale of the same or closely related products in different markets to take advantage of price disparity between the two markets".

Core Strategy

Meyer Weinstein in his 1931 book Arbitrage in Securities describes convertible arbitrage as the opportunity to exploit the non-linear relationship of the convertible with respect to the underlying stock.

This can be represented in the instance of the price of the convertible and the underlying stock not moving in unison. That is to say that one of the securities is rising while the other is falling or vice versa. This allows for the investor to take a long position in the convertible and a short position in the underlying.

When the convertible security is selling close to its investment value, and the underlying is not at a great discount, the investor may buy the convertible and sell one-half of the short stock. This allows the investor to be long and short at the same time. Essentially allowing the investor to hedge against both a rise and fall of the stock, and any rise in the convertible to be taken as a profit (Weinstein, Arbitrage in Securities, p. 151).

Weinstein is outlining a convertible "market-neutral" hedge which still remains prevalent in the current-day convertible arbitrage strageties.
